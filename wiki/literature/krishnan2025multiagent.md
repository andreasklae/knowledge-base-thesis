# Krishnan (2025) — Advancing Multi-Agent Systems Through Model Context Protocol

## Summary

A 118-page comprehensive survey-style paper proposing a framework for multi-agent systems built on the **Model Context Protocol (MCP)**. Krishnan extends prior work (his own "AI Agents: Evolution, Architecture, and Real-World Applications", arXiv:2503.12687) by adding multi-agent coordination, advanced context management techniques, and three implementation case studies (Enterprise Knowledge Management, Collaborative Research Assistant, Distributed Problem-Solving). The paper's central problem framing — borrowed from Microsoft's Sam Schillace — is the **"disconnected models problem"**: *"To be autonomous you have to carry context through a bunch of actions, but the models are very disconnected and don't have continuity the way we do."* The proposed solution: standardised context sharing via MCP plus structured coordination protocols, with reported performance gains of 27-83% across multiple metrics versus baseline approaches.

**Critical reading note.** The paper reads more like a vision/architecture document with claimed performance numbers than a peer-reviewed empirical study. The reported metrics (e.g., 55.8% reduction in coordination conflicts, 4.7x faster than human teams) are presented without methodological detail sufficient to verify. Treat as a *conceptual* reference for MCP-based multi-agent design patterns, not as empirical evidence.

## Main claims

### The disconnected models problem (Section 1)

The paper's central problem framing:
- **Context discontinuity across agent boundaries** — information generated by one agent doesn't transfer effectively to others.
- **Temporal discontinuity** — agents don't remember past decisions; can't build on previous work.
- **Contextual prioritization breakdown** — no effective mechanism to determine relevance.
- **Cross-modal context integration failures** — agents struggle to integrate text, images, structured data into coherent understanding.

These four challenges are claimed to limit current multi-agent effectiveness. The thesis can adopt this taxonomy when discussing multi-agent failure modes.

### The MCP solution architecture (Sections 3-4)

- **Client-server architecture.** AI models (clients) ↔ data sources/tools (servers). JSON-RPC 2.0 over the wire. Separation of concerns.
- **Server-side primitives:** Prompts, Resources, Tools.
- **Client-side primitives:** Roots (data realm entry points with permission boundaries), Sampling (servers can request completions from the model).
- **Methods:** `prompt.list/get`, `resource.list/get`, `tool.list/describe/execute`, `root.list/describe`, `sample.generate`.
- **Reference multi-agent architecture (Section 4.1):** Agent Runtime Environment + Agent Instances + Context Management Layer + Coordination Framework + External Integration Layer + Security/Governance Layer.

### Multi-agent coordination patterns (Section 4.3)

- **Task decomposition:** Hierarchical Task Networks, Contract Net Protocol, Role-Based Assignment, Emergent Assignment.
- **Progress monitoring:** Status Reporting, Dependency Management, Synchronization Points, Adaptive Scheduling.
- **Failure recovery:** Failure Detection, Recovery Strategies, Exception Escalation, Learning from Failures.
- **Resource management:** Priority-Based Allocation, Adaptive Scaling, Resource Reservation, Fairness Enforcement.

### Advanced context management (Section 5)

- **Context persistence:** Hierarchical Storage (hot/warm/cold/archival), Semantic Knowledge Graphs, Embedding-Based Representations, Event Streams.
- **Context retrieval:** Multi-Stage Retrieval Pipelines, Hybrid Models (keyword + semantic), Contextual Relevance Models (topical + temporal + authority + utility + novelty).
- **Forgetting strategies:** Utility-Based Retention, Importance-Weighted Decay, Summarization, Knowledge Distillation.
- **Context prioritization:** Multi-Factor Importance Models, Graph-Based Centrality, Utility Estimation, Learning-Based Models.

### Three case studies (Section 6)

1. **Enterprise Knowledge Management System (EKMS)** — hierarchical multi-agent architecture for 50,000-employee multinational. Reported: ECE 67% improvement in query response, 78% adoption.
2. **Collaborative Research Assistant (CRA)** — peer-based architecture for interdisciplinary research teams. Reported: 2.7x increase in hypothesis generation, 41% increase in manuscript output.
3. **Distributed Problem-Solving System (DPSS)** — flexible architecture for engineering teams. Reported: 34% solution quality improvement, 58% time reduction.

### Claimed quantitative results (Section 7.3)

- Cross-domain synthesis: 78.3% accuracy vs 61.5% RAG vs 53.2% single-agent.
- Coordination overhead reduced 47%.
- Context retention across sessions: 83.7% vs 42.3% baseline.
- 4.7x faster than human teams on knowledge-intensive tasks (with 83% of human team quality).

## Method / evidence

- **No experimental code or reproducible benchmarks released** in the paper itself.
- The "evaluations" are reported as comparative percentages against vaguely defined baselines without published methodology, datasets, or replication artifacts.
- Three case studies presented at architecture level with deployment metrics but no source code or measurement protocol.
- Extensive citation list (118 references) but most cited works are dated 2023-2024 and many are paraphrased without direct quotation.

## Relevance to this thesis

### 1. Useful taxonomy of MCP-based multi-agent design patterns

For thesis arguments about *how MCP enables multi-agent coordination*, Krishnan provides a reasonably complete taxonomy: server primitives, client primitives, task decomposition patterns, coordination protocols. The thesis can adopt this vocabulary even while treating the empirical claims with skepticism.

### 2. The "disconnected models problem" framing

Schillace's quote (*"To be autonomous you have to carry context through a bunch of actions, but the models are very disconnected"*) is a quotable industry articulation of the thesis's central concern. The thesis can use this framing.

### 3. Reference architecture as a comparison point

Krishnan's reference architecture (6 layers, multiple specialised agent types) is one specific decomposition. The thesis's [[framework-four-components]] is a different decomposition (workspace/tools/skills/data). Worth comparing the two as alternative ways of partitioning the multi-agent design space.

### 4. Context as a first-class infrastructure concern

Krishnan's repeated emphasis on context management as the central multi-agent challenge aligns with the thesis's emphasis on infrastructure-as-bottleneck. [[context-engineering]] concept page can cite Krishnan's taxonomy of context types (temporal, spatial, task, social, domain, personal, interaction).

### 5. Cautionary methodological example

The thesis's empirical work should be more rigorously documented than this paper. The reported numbers in Krishnan have no clear methodology behind them; the thesis should aim higher.

## Tensions and qualifications

- **Empirical claims unverified.** The paper reports specific percentages (e.g., 47% coordination overhead reduction) without published methodology. The thesis should not cite these numbers as established fact.
- **Reads as architectural advocacy, not research.** The structure (challenges → MCP solution → claimed metrics → industry transformation) is closer to a vision document than a research paper. Treat as such.
- **Author has commercial/consulting motivations.** Krishnan publishes prolifically on AI agent architectures; the framing is consistent with someone advocating for MCP adoption.
- **MCP-monism.** The paper treats MCP as the solution to essentially all multi-agent coordination problems. Other approaches (centralised orchestration, message-passing without context sharing, blackboard architectures) get less attention than they deserve.
- **Case studies are unverifiable.** The three case studies describe enterprise deployments with reported metrics but no organisation names, public reports, or replication paths.
- **Reference list is heavy on 2023-2024 ML papers** but light on classical multi-agent systems literature (Jennings, Wooldridge, etc.) and computer science fundamentals (consensus protocols, distributed systems). The treatment of these classical foundations is shallow.
