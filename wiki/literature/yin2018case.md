# Yin (2018) — Case Study Research and Applications: Design and Methods (6th ed.)

## Summary

The canonical methodology textbook on **case study research** as a formal social-science research method. Yin (PhD experimental psychology, founder of COSMOS Corporation) has been writing and refining this book since 1984; the sixth edition adds 11 worked applications. The book makes one core argument repeatedly: **case study research is a distinct, legitimate mode of social-science inquiry — not "soft" research, not a substitute when randomised trials are infeasible, not just exploratory pilot work — when *and only when* it is done with methodic rigour.** Yin's twofold definition: a case study (1) *empirically investigates a contemporary phenomenon in depth and within its real-world context, especially when the boundaries between phenomenon and context are not clearly evident*, and (2) *copes with the technically distinctive situation in which there will be many more variables of interest than data points*, benefits from prior theoretical propositions, and relies on multiple triangulating sources of evidence. The book covers design (single vs multiple, holistic vs embedded), preparation (protocol, pilot, training), six sources of evidence (documentation, archival records, interviews, direct observation, participant-observation, physical artefacts), four data-collection principles (multiple sources, database, chain of evidence, social-media care), five analytic techniques (pattern matching, explanation building, time-series, logic models, cross-case synthesis), and reporting. For the thesis, this is the **canonical methodology reference for case-study-shaped research designs** — relevant because the thesis's empirical investigations are case-study-shaped (single experimental settings explored in depth) rather than RCT-shaped.

## Main claims

### 1. The "trilogy" — case study research / case studies / case(s)

A central conceptual clarification in the 6th edition:
- **Case study research** = the *mode of inquiry*.
- **Case studies** = the *method* (research method used in doing case study research).
- **Case(s)** = the *unit* of inquiry within a case study.

This distinguishes *research case studies* from "popular" case studies (journalism) and "teaching-practice" case studies (HBS-style), which are the broader cultural references and which often **taint perceptions of case study research as a research method**. Yin's central preface argument: most disparagement of case-study research is rooted in confusion with these non-research uses.

### 2. The twofold definition

**Scope:**
- Empirical method.
- Investigates a *contemporary* phenomenon (the case) in *depth* within its *real-world context*.
- Especially when *boundaries between phenomenon and context are not clearly evident*.

**Features:**
- Many more variables of interest than data points.
- Benefits from *prior theoretical propositions* to guide design, data collection, analysis.
- Relies on *multiple sources of evidence* with data *converging in a triangulating fashion*.

The distinguishing feature: the case study deliberately *does not separate phenomenon from context* (as experiments do) and *deals with contemporary, not historical events* (as history does), with richer context than surveys can carry.

### 3. Five components of a case study research design

1. **Study questions** — typically "how" and "why" questions.
2. **Study propositions** (when applicable) — directs attention to what to examine; some exploratory studies have no propositions but should still state purpose, criteria for success.
3. **Case(s) to be studied** — the unit of analysis; defining and bounding the case is treated as load-bearing.
4. **Logic linking data to propositions** — foreshadows analysis techniques.
5. **Criteria for interpreting findings** — for case study research, *rival explanations* play the role that statistical significance plays in quantitative work. The more rivals addressed and rejected, the stronger the findings.

### 4. Quality criteria for case study research design

Four classical validity criteria translated for case studies:
- **Construct validity** — operationalising the concepts being studied. Improved by multiple sources, chain of evidence, key informant review.
- **Internal validity** (for explanatory case studies) — pattern matching, explanation building, addressing rivals, using logic models.
- **External validity** — *analytic* generalisation to theory, *not* statistical generalisation to populations.
- **Reliability** — protocol, database; "another investigator could repeat your procedure and arrive at the same findings."

### 5. Analytic generalisation, NOT statistical generalisation

This is Yin's most important single methodological claim and his most-quoted point:

- **Statistical generalisation** infers from sample to population. Case studies are *not* samples and cannot be statistically generalised. *"A fatal flaw in doing case studies is to consider statistical generalization to be the way of generalizing the findings."*
- **Analytic generalisation** generalises *findings to theory* — extending, refining, or rejecting the theoretical propositions that motivated the case. The case study is *generalisable to theoretical propositions, not to populations or universes.*
- The analogy: nobody asks "how do you generalise from a single experiment?" — generalisation in experimental science comes from *replicating the same phenomenon under different conditions* (theoretical replication), not from sampling.
- Yin's "two-level" inference diagram (Figure 2.2): case findings → theoretical propositions → other concrete situations.

### 6. Single-case rationales (5) and multiple-case logic

Single-case rationales:
1. **Critical** case — testing a well-formulated theory.
2. **Unusual** case — common conditions don't apply.
3. **Common** case — representative of an everyday situation.
4. **Revelatory** case — observing a phenomenon previously inaccessible.
5. **Longitudinal** case — studying same case at multiple points in time.

Multiple-case designs follow **replication logic**, not sampling logic:
- **Literal replication** — cases predicted to produce similar results.
- **Theoretical replication** — cases predicted to produce contrasting results for predictable reasons.

Each case is a discrete experiment; multiple cases extend theory.

### 7. Six sources of evidence

- **Documentation** (letters, agendas, administrative documents, news articles).
- **Archival records** (service records, organisational charts, budgets, survey data).
- **Interviews** (prolonged conversations, focused interviews, structured interviews).
- **Direct observations** (formal or casual, often during field visits).
- **Participant-observation** (researcher takes a role in the situation studied).
- **Physical artefacts** (technological devices, tools, instruments, works of art).

### 8. Four principles of data collection

1. **Use multiple sources of evidence** — *triangulation* of data sources, investigators, theories, methods. Construct validity rises with convergence.
2. **Create a case study database** — separates the *evidence* from the *report*, so others can review the raw materials.
3. **Maintain a chain of evidence** — explicit links between questions → protocol → citations to specific evidence → case study database → ultimately to the case study report.
4. **Exercise care when using social media data** — new principle in the 6th edition. Authenticity, representativeness, anonymity, and ethics concerns.

### 9. Five analytic techniques

1. **Pattern matching** — compare empirically-observed pattern with predicted pattern. Strongest if patterns are precise.
2. **Explanation building** — iterative; especially for explanatory studies.
3. **Time-series analysis** — single or multiple time series, chronologies.
4. **Logic models** — staged sequences of cause-effect-cause-effect over time.
5. **Cross-case synthesis** — for multiple-case studies; treat each case as a separate study, aggregate findings.

Four general strategies that *underlie* the techniques:
- Relying on theoretical propositions.
- Working data from "the ground up."
- Developing a case description.
- Examining plausible rival explanations (transversal across all the others).

### 10. Plausible rival explanations as core quality control

Donald Campbell's foreword and Yin's repeated emphasis: *"the core of the scientific method is not experimentation per se but rather the strategy connoted by the phrase 'plausible rival hypotheses.'"*

For case study research, the strategy is:
- Identify plausible rivals upfront, *before* data collection (so you can collect data about them).
- Seek evidence for *and against* each rival.
- The strength of findings is measured by the rivals ruled out.

### 11. Reporting

Six compositional structures, choice depends on audience and purpose:
- Linear-analytic (problem → method → findings → conclusions).
- Comparative (same case re-examined from different perspectives).
- Chronological.
- Theory-building.
- "Suspense" structure (conclusion first).
- Unsequenced (subsection order doesn't matter).

An "exemplary" case study is: *significant, complete, considers alternative perspectives, displays sufficient evidence, engagingly composed.*

## Method / evidence

- **Authoritative textbook**, sixth edition (first edition 1984). 100,000+ Google Scholar citations make it the second-most-cited methodology book in social sciences.
- Built on Yin's personal research practice, Campbell's foreword endorsing it, and 11 worked applications across organisational studies, evaluation, urban affairs.
- Foreword by Donald T. Campbell, who positions it in the tradition of *plausible rival hypotheses* as the core of scientific method.
- The 6th edition adds applications and updated treatment of analytic generalisation, cross-case syntheses, mixed-methods integration.

## Relevance to this thesis

### 1. Methodological anchor for the thesis's empirical investigations

The thesis investigates agent productivity through *experimental setups in specific organisational settings* — not RCTs, not survey research. **These are case studies in Yin's sense:** real-world phenomena (agent productivity) in real-world contexts (Riksantikvaren, etc.), with many variables and few "data points," with theoretical propositions about infrastructure-as-bottleneck guiding the design. The thesis should explicitly position its empirical work as case-study research per Yin.

### 2. Analytic generalisation as the thesis's epistemic move

The thesis cannot statistically generalise from a handful of agent experiments to "all agents in all organisations." Yin's distinction is the right move: **generalise to theory** (the four-component framework, the calibration thread, the deterministic-tools hypothesis) **not to a population of organisations.** The case studies serve as theoretical replications.

### 3. Plausible rival explanations as a methodological discipline

Most thesis-relevant single takeaway. Every empirical finding the thesis reports — "the workspace reset reduced sycophancy by X%," "the deterministic verifier closed the calibration gap" — needs an explicit rival-explanation analysis. *What else could explain this result?* Model variance? Prompt-specific artefacts? Selection effects? Order effects? **Yin's framework formalises this as a quality-control discipline.**

### 4. The four-case multiple-case logic maps onto the thesis's experiment suite

The thesis runs multiple experiments (math, Riksantikvaren, interviews, etc.). Yin's **replication logic** is the right framing:
- *Literal* replications: experiments designed to produce similar findings.
- *Theoretical* replications: experiments designed to produce contrasting findings (e.g. high-context-engineering vs no-context-engineering).

This is more rigorous than "we ran several experiments." Worth lifting into the thesis's methodology chapter.

### 5. Construct validity for "agent productivity"

Yin's framework demands operational definitions of constructs. "Productivity" is the thesis's central construct, and the multi-faceted nature (speed, correctness, calibration, diversity, etc.) makes it construct-validity-critical. **The thesis's productivity definition should pass Yin's construct-validity tests:** multiple operationalisations, multiple sources, chain of evidence.

### 6. Case study database and chain of evidence as research artefacts

The thesis's knowledge base (this wiki + experimental code/data) *is* a Yin-style case study database. The wiki's literature pages, concept pages, work pages, log, index — they are the chain of evidence from raw experimental data to written conclusions. The thesis can describe its own research infrastructure using Yin's vocabulary.

### 7. The "research vs non-research" distinction protects the thesis's empirical posture

Yin's preface warning — that popular case studies and teaching cases blur the perception of case-study research as a method — is directly applicable. The thesis's empirical investigations should be *explicitly methodically described* (protocol, evidence, analysis technique, rivals considered) to differentiate them from anecdotal vibes-coding-style narratives.

### 8. Single-case rationales as a vocabulary for selection

If the thesis includes a Riksantikvaren-specific case, Yin's vocabulary lets the thesis frame it as a *revelatory* case (previously inaccessible) or *critical* case (testing a well-formulated theory) — and justify the choice.

## Tensions and qualifications

- **Long and methodologically conservative.** The book is 25+ years in development and dense; some pedagogical sections (preparing the researcher, ethics, IRB) are not directly relevant to a CS-flavoured thesis.
- **Social-science vocabulary.** Yin's terminology (construct validity, etc.) imports concepts from sociology and program evaluation. Mapping these to CS/HCI experimentation is straightforward but not always frictionless.
- **Limited treatment of computational/digital methods.** The 6th edition adds a Principle 4 on social media data but doesn't deeply engage with the methodological challenges of LLM-mediated research, log/trace data analysis, or computational replication. The thesis should cite Yin for the *framework* but augment with newer methodological work for the *practice*.
- **"Plausible rival explanations" can be unbounded.** Yin admits this — there are no formal procedures for *rigorously testing* rivals; the practitioner judges plausibility. This is a real epistemic limit that the thesis should acknowledge when claiming rivals have been ruled out.
- **External validity through analytic generalisation is contested.** Some methodologists (and reviewers) still expect statistical-style external validity. Defending the analytic-generalisation move requires the thesis to explicitly cite Yin and Campbell.
- **Construct validity for AI experiments is hard.** "Productivity," "infrastructure quality," "context engineering" are theory-laden constructs. Yin's framework demands operational definitions; the thesis must work to deliver them.
- **Mixed methods are addressed but lightly.** The thesis combines case-study evidence with experimental measurements (P(True), task completion, accuracy). Yin endorses mixed-methods integration but doesn't deeply specify how case-study data and quantitative measurements should be integrated.
- **Reliability is hard for LLM-mediated experiments.** Stochasticity, version drift, prompt sensitivity make exact replication difficult. Yin's reliability principle (another investigator could repeat the procedure and arrive at the same findings) needs translation for LLM experiments — perhaps as *protocol reproducibility* rather than result reproducibility.
