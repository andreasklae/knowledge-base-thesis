# Scope and Limitations

Explicit limits committed to in the extended description.

## What this thesis is

A **single-developer, multi-case investigation with hypothesis-generating ambition.** Six experiments, each exercising the four-component framework from a different angle.

## What this thesis is not

- **Not a full user study with statistical power.** The cases are not powered to support quantitative claims about a population of developers, agents, or tasks.
- **Not a multi-developer comparison of AI-assisted productivity.** That comparison is a different thesis and is left to future work.
- **Not a formal evaluation with strong external validity.** Generalisation is analytic, not statistical.

## The framework's claim, if supported

That the patterns named — workspace, tools, skills, data; calibration as a systems property; the deterministic-tools hypothesis — are real and useful, and that they hold across the cases studied. Quantitative generalisation to broader populations is left to future work.

## The methodological frame

Following Yin (2018) on case-study research, the contribution is **analytic rather than statistical.** The cases test whether the framework's decomposition holds and is useful across distinct agent-infrastructure problems, not whether a measured effect generalises to a population. See [../decisions/2026-05-13-methodology-design-science.md](../decisions/2026-05-13-methodology-design-science.md) for the design-science framing within which this sits.

## Why naming these limits matters

A thesis that claims more than its design supports loses credibility on the claims it can actually support. Naming the limits at the outset is a methodological commitment — it tells the reader what to expect and what *not* to expect, and it constrains the writing process to stay within the design.
