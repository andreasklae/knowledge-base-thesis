# Methodology: Design Science Research

## Context

The thesis builds and evaluates agent systems across six experiments. The choice of methodological frame shapes what counts as a contribution, how cases are constructed, and how the framework is evaluated. Two practitioner interviews (Erfan Nariman; the Sequoia–Karpathy interview) raise a related question: are they evidence or are they directional inspiration?

## Decision

The primary methodological frame is **design science research** (Hevner, March, Park, Ram, 2004). The agent systems built across the experiments are design artefacts whose construction generates research findings; the framework itself is the meta-artefact whose evaluation across multiple cases is the contribution.

Generalisation is **analytic, not statistical**, following Yin (2018) on case-study research. The cases test whether the framework's decomposition holds and is useful across distinct agent-infrastructure problems — not whether a measured effect generalises to a population. See [`../manuscript-notes/scope-and-limitations.md`](../manuscript-notes/scope-and-limitations.md).

**Practitioner interviews are inspirational and directional, not statistically or analytically evidential.** Specifically:

- The Erfan Nariman interview (April–May 2026) is used to motivate the project and to expose the framework to a practitioner whose pushback can sharpen the claims. Observations from it act as throughline references in the Skills, Knowledge, and Sleep chapters.
- The Sequoia–Karpathy interview is treated as a practitioner source and is used where Karpathy's framing of agents, verification, coding, and model behaviour clarifies the thesis argument.

Neither interview is presented as evidence with statistical or analytic weight. Individual practitioner accounts cannot bear that load, and claiming otherwise would be methodologically dishonest.

## Consequences

- Every experiment is reported with explicit scope and limitations. Negative results are findings, not failures.
- The framework's value is in being a useful vocabulary across cases, not in measuring an average effect.
- The thesis cannot claim that "skills produce X% productivity improvement" in a population sense. It can claim "in these cases, skills produced the following effects, and the framework predicted the direction of those effects."
- Interview material informs experiment design and chapter framing but does not appear as data in results sections.
